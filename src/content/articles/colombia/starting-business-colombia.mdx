Colombia has quietly become one of the more foreigner-friendly places in Latin America to actually run a business. The bureaucracy can be genuinely exhausting — nobody's pretending otherwise — but the legal framework is more accessible than most people expect, and the costs are low enough that you don't need to be sitting on a pile of capital to get started.

This guide walks you through the full process of forming a company in Colombia as a foreigner in 2026: the entity types, the registration steps, the real costs, and the mistakes that catch people off guard.

## Which Business Entity Should You Use?

There are several legal structures available in Colombia — *Empresa Unipersonal*, *Sociedad Limitada* (SRL), *Sociedad Anónima* (SA), and others — but the overwhelming majority of foreigners (and Colombians, for that matter) form a **SAS: Sociedad por Acciones Simplificada**.

The SAS was introduced in 2008 and essentially replaced every other structure for small and medium businesses overnight. Here's why it dominates:

- **One shareholder minimum** — you can be the sole owner
- **Limited liability** — your personal assets are protected from company debts
- **No minimum capital requirement** — you can technically form one with 1 COP
- **Flexible corporate governance** — no requirement for a board of directors or *revisor fiscal* (statutory auditor) unless you hit certain revenue thresholds
- **Fast to form** — in theory, you can do it in a single day at the Cámara de Comercio

For a freelancer turning their operation into a proper company, a solo founder building a startup, or a foreign investor setting up a local entity, the SAS is almost always the right call.

## Do You Need a Cédula de Extranjería?

This is one of the most common misconceptions. **No — you do not need a cédula de extranjería to form a SAS.** A valid passport is sufficient to be a shareholder and legal representative of a Colombian company. You'll use your passport number as your identification throughout the registration process.

That said, if you're planning to live and work in Colombia long-term, getting a cédula de extranjería (the ID card issued to foreign residents with valid visas) makes everything considerably smoother — particularly for banking.

## Step-by-Step: Forming Your SAS

### Step 1 — Draft the Articles of Incorporation

You'll need a *documento de constitución* (articles of incorporation). This is a private document — no notary required, which saves you time and money compared to older entity types. It needs to include:

- Company name and registered address
- Corporate purpose (keep this broad — you can list multiple activities)
- Share capital structure
- Name and ID of the legal representative
- Duration of the company (most people put *indefinida*)

You can write this yourself using templates available on the Cámara de Comercio website, but if your Spanish isn't strong or you want it done properly, a lawyer will charge roughly **300,000–600,000 COP** for this document.

### Step 2 — Register at the Cámara de Comercio

Take your articles of incorporation to your local Cámara de Comercio. In Medellín, the main office is in El Centro on Calle 53 with Carrera 49; in Bogotá, the most accessible branch for expats is usually the one in Chapinero on Carrera 7. You can also initiate the process online through the **Empresa en Línea** portal (empresaenlinea.com.co), though foreigners sometimes hit snags there and end up going in person anyway.

Registration fees are calculated based on your declared capital. For a company formed with minimal capital (say, 1,000,000–5,000,000 COP), you're looking at roughly **250,000–450,000 COP** in total Cámara fees, which includes the matricula mercantil (business registration) and the initial annual renewal fee.

Bring your passport, the signed articles of incorporation, and cash or a debit card. Some branches have payment terminals; others don't.

### Step 3 — Register with DIAN and Obtain Your RUT

The **RUT (Registro Único Tributario)** is your company's tax ID, issued by the DIAN (Colombia's tax authority). Without it, you can't invoice legally, open a business bank account, or operate properly.

Register at dian.gov.co or visit a DIAN office directly. In practice, many expats find the online portal frustrating and end up going to a DIAN *punto de contacto* in person. Bring your Cámara de Comercio registration certificate, your passport, and the completed RUT form (Form 001).

The RUT itself is **free**, but plan to spend half a day on this. DIAN offices move at their own pace.

At registration, you'll declare your *actividades económicas* using CIIU codes (Colombia's version of industry classification codes). Get these right — they affect your IVA obligations and tax regime. An accountant is worth their fee here.

### Step 4 — Open a Business Bank Account

This is where things get genuinely annoying. Colombian banks are conservative with foreigners, and many will turn you away if you don't have a cédula de extranjería. **Bancolombia** and **Banco de Bogotá** are the most commonly used for business accounts, but both may ask for residency documentation.

If you're working on a tourist visa or tourist stamp, you're likely to struggle. Some expats have had success with **Nequi** (Bancolombia's digital wallet) as a stopgap, though it's not a proper business account. **Nubank Colombia** has become increasingly popular and is more foreigner-friendly, but check current requirements as policies shift.

Realistically, having a *cédula de extranjería* — which requires a valid visa — makes bank account opening dramatically easier.

### Step 5 — Set Up Facturación Electrónica

Since 2020, virtually all Colombian businesses are required to issue **facturas electrónicas** (electronic invoices) through DIAN-authorised software providers. You cannot legally bill clients with a PDF or a handwritten invoice.

Popular platforms include **Siigo**, **Alegra**, and **Bsale**. Alegra tends to be the most expat-friendly in terms of interface. Costs run around **80,000–200,000 COP per month** depending on the plan and transaction volume. Your accountant will typically help you set this up.

## Costs: What Does It Actually Cost?

Here's a realistic breakdown for forming and running a basic SAS in 2026:

**One-time formation costs:**
- Articles of incorporation (lawyer): 300,000–600,000 COP
- Cámara de Comercio registration: 250,000–450,000 COP
- RUT registration: Free
- Invoicing software setup: Often included in first month

**Monthly ongoing costs:**
- Accountant (*contador público*): 400,000–1,200,000 COP/month
- Facturación electrónica software: 80,000–200,000 COP/month
- Cámara de Comercio annual renewal: ~250,000–400,000 COP/year (paid in March)

A *contador público* (certified public accountant) is not optional — you are legally required to have one associated with your company. Budget for at least 500,000 COP/month for a decent freelance contador who handles your monthly declarations and keeps you out of trouble with DIAN.

## Tax Obligations

A Colombian SAS is subject to several taxes:

- **Impuesto de Renta** — corporate income tax, currently 35% on net profits
- **IVA (VAT)** — 19% on most goods and services. Declared bi-monthly if you're under certain thresholds, monthly if you're above
- **ICA (Impuesto de Industria y Comercio)** — a municipal tax paid to your city government, typically 0.3%–1.4% of gross revenue depending on your activity and city
- **Retención en la fuente** — a withholding tax system where clients may deduct a percentage (typically 3.5%–11%) from payments to you at source

This is exactly why a good accountant isn't a luxury — it's infrastructure.

## Visa Implications: The Tipo M (Migrant) Visa

Owning a SAS doesn't automatically give you the right to live and work in Colombia. If you want a long-term legal status tied to your business, you'll want a **Visa Tipo M — Inversión** (Migrant Investor Visa).

To qualify, you generally need to demonstrate an investment in your company of at least **100 times the monthly minimum wage** (salario mínimo), which in 2026 works out to roughly **130,000,000–140,000,000 COP** depending on the current minimum wage. This can be demonstrated through your company's capitalisation.

There's also a **Visa Tipo M — Independiente**, aimed at freelancers and self-employed foreigners, which has different requirements. Consult an immigration lawyer before assuming you qualify — the goalposts shift.

## Hiring Employees

Colombian labour law is employee-friendly and the costs are real. On top of any agreed salary, an employer must contribute to:

- **Prestaciones sociales** — roughly 21.83% of salary (cesantías, prima, vacaciones, etc.)
- **Seguridad social** — health (EPS) and pension contributions, totalling around 20.5% employer contribution
- **Parafiscales** — SENA, ICBF, caja de compensación familiar, totalling about 9% (though small companies under certain thresholds may be exempt from some of these)

In practice, hiring someone on a formal *contrato de trabajo* costs you roughly **1.4–1.6 times their base salary** in total. Factor this in from the start.

### The *Contrato de Prestación de Servicios* Alternative

Many foreigners — and Colombians — use a **contrato de prestación de servicios** (services contract) instead of a formal employment contract. This is essentially a freelance/consultant arrangement where the contractor is responsible for their own social security contributions. It's legal when used correctly, but using it to disguise what is effectively a full-time employment relationship is called *contratación laboral disfrazada* and carries real legal risk.

## Common Mistakes Foreigners Make

**Using a tourist stamp to open a company and then trying to run it actively.** You can legally own shares in a Colombian company as a tourist, but actually working within it, signing contracts, or conducting business operations on Colombian soil without a work-authorised visa is legally murky at best.

**Skipping the accountant to save money.** DIAN fines are not small. A single missed declaration can cost you more than a year's accountant fees.

**Declaring too narrow a corporate purpose.** If your SAS is registered only for "software development" and you want to invoice for consulting, you may face complications. Keep your CIIU codes broad.

**Ignoring ICA.** Many expats focus entirely on DIAN and forget that each municipality has its own tax authority. In Medellín, this is handled through the Secretaría de Hacienda.

## Finding Good Lawyers and Accountants

The most reliable way to find a good Colombian lawyer or accountant as a foreigner is through **expat community referrals** — Facebook groups like *Expats in Medellín* or *Bogotá Expats* are genuinely useful here. Ask specifically for someone who has worked with foreign-owned SAS companies.

Look for a *contador público* who is registered with the **CTCP** (the national accounting standards body) and ask to see their professional licence (*tarjeta profesional*). For lawyers, check registration with the *Consejo Superior de la Judicatura*.

Expect to pay **150,000–350,000 COP per hour** for a good business lawyer in Bogotá or Medellín. Outside the major cities, rates are lower but so is the pool of specialists.
